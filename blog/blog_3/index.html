Ocular Dominance Columns: Example

Figure 1. Ocular dominance columns (see red and blue stripes) in the primary visual cortex (V1). The V1/V2 border is shown as white ribbon above the surface. A more detailed description of the surface and how it relates to an actual human brain can be found in the text body. Note that there is a pause in the middle of the video for a better view on V1.

In my PhD project, I am mainly interested in high-resolution fMRI methods and applications. Therefore, ocular dominance columns (ODCs) are also part of my studies as they have been for many other researchers as well.

In this blog post, I show a small excerpt of my work on mapping ODCs in living humans using high-resolution fMRI at 7 T. Main part of the project is the comparison between different fMRI acquisition strategies with the aim to map ODCs with high specificity across cortical depth. You can find more information in my talk at the ISMRM 2020.

The above figure illustrates an exemplary ODC map from one participant acquired with GE-BOLD and isotropic 0.8 mm resolution. The color overlay shows z-scores of the differential contrast (left eye stimulation > right eye stimulation). Please note the red and blue stripes (corresponding to locations with either left or right eye dominance) which are exclusively found in the primary visual cortex (V1). The underlying surface mesh seems a little bit odd and does not look like a normal human brain. This is because we defined the cortical surface mesh in the functional data itself. Since we imaged a coronal-oblique slab which only covers the back of the brain, the surface mesh is only defined for this part of the brain as well and is shown here as inflated mesh for better visualization. When the video starts, you look at the medial side. The mesh is rotated by 90 degrees so that the anterior-to-posterior and dorsal-to-ventral directions are from bottom-to-top and left-to-right in the image, respectively. V1 is on the right side and the V1/V2 border is shown as white ribbon above the mesh and is based on a separate retinotopy scan.
